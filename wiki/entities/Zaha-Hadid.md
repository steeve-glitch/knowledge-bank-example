Iraqi-British architect (1950–2016). One of the most influential architects of the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries, known for fluid, curvilinear buildings that appear to defy gravity. Founded and led [[Zaha-Hadid-Architects]] with partner Patrik Schumacher.

Hadid studied at the Architectural Association in London under Rem Koolhaas and Elia Zenghelis. She spent nearly twenty years as "the paper architect" — producing visionary, largely unbuilt work — before completing her first major building, the Vitra Fire Station, in 1993. Her later career demonstrated that the drawing practice had been foundational: it generated a formal vocabulary that became buildable as digital fabrication technologies advanced.

She was a pioneer of [[parametric-design]], using computational tools to produce complex curved forms impossible to specify by hand. Her practice faced criticism for prioritising spectacle over function, and for labour conditions on Qatari construction projects — criticisms she largely rejected.

**Awards:** Pritzker Prize (2004, first woman and first Arab to win); RIBA Royal Gold Medal (2016, first woman to win, awarded posthumously).
