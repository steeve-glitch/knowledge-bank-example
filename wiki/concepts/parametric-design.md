---
title: Parametric Design
aliases: [parametricism, computational design, algorithmic design]
tags: [design-method, digital-fabrication, form-generation]
---

A design approach in which the shape and structure of a building (or object) is generated by a set of rules or algorithms rather than drawn by hand. The designer sets the parameters — constraints like site boundaries, structural loads, or desired spatial relationships — and software explores and generates forms that satisfy them. Because the computer handles the geometry, parametric design makes it practical to work with complex curves, branching structures, and non-repeating surfaces that would be prohibitively difficult to specify manually.

The term "parametricism" was coined by Patrik Schumacher of [[Zaha-Hadid-Architects]] in a 2008 manifesto, where he argued it represented a new architectural style suited to the complexity of contemporary urban life. Critics have argued the label overstates the coherence of the approach and that "parametric" describes a tool rather than a style.

Parametric design is closely associated with digital fabrication: CNC milling, robotic arm construction, and 3D printing make it possible to actually build the complex geometries the software generates. This is why the approach became viable in the 2000s rather than earlier — the fabrication technology had to catch up with the design ambition.

## Examples

From ingested sources:

- **[[Zaha-Hadid]]** used parametric tools extensively from the late 1990s onward, developing the approach with Patrik Schumacher. The MAXXI Museum in Rome and the Heydar Aliyev Center in Baku are considered its fullest built expressions — buildings where interior and exterior surfaces flow continuously without visible joints or transitions.
