## Summary

Zaha Hadid (1950–2016) was an Iraqi-British architect widely regarded as one of the most radical and influential designers of the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries. Born in Baghdad, she studied at the Architectural Association in London under Rem Koolhaas and Elia Zenghelis, and spent decades producing visionary competition entries and theoretical drawings before completing her first major building — the Vitra Fire Station in Weil am Rhein, Germany — in 1993. Her early reputation rested almost entirely on unbuilt work, earning her the sobriquet "the paper architect," yet the intensity of her drawing practice proved foundational: it forced a rigorous exploration of form that later became buildable as digital fabrication technologies caught up with her imagination.

Hadid's signature vocabulary — fluid curves, intersecting planes, structures that appear to defy gravity — drew from multiple intellectual sources. She was deeply influenced by Russian Suprematism, particularly the paintings of Kazimir Malevich, whose fragmented geometries she reinterpreted in three dimensions. She was also an early adopter of computational design tools, working with her longtime collaborator Patrik Schumacher to develop what they called "parametric design": using algorithms to generate and refine complex forms that would be impossible to specify by hand. The MAXXI National Museum of Contemporary Arts in Rome (2009) and the Heydar Aliyev Center in Baku (2012) are considered the fullest expressions of this approach — buildings in which the exterior and interior flow into each other without visible breaks, and where the architecture itself becomes the exhibit.

Her career was not without controversy. Critics accused her of producing buildings that prioritised spectacle over function, and of being difficult to work with — accusations she herself acknowledged and largely dismissed. More seriously, her firm faced criticism over the labour conditions of migrant workers on the Al Wakrah stadium in Qatar, a project she defended by arguing that architects have limited authority over construction contracts. She was also the first woman and the first Arab to receive the Pritzker Architecture Prize (2004), the field's highest honour, and the first woman to receive the Royal Institute of British Architects' Royal Gold Medal (2016, awarded weeks before her death from a heart attack at age 65).

Her death prompted an outpouring of tributes that testified to the scale of her influence: she had, over four decades, fundamentally expanded the vocabulary of what a building could look like. Less discussed but perhaps more significant is what her practice demonstrated about the relationship between representation and construction — that the act of drawing, at sufficient intensity, can create the future it imagines.

## Key Points

- Born Baghdad 1950; studied at AA London under Rem Koolhaas; died March 2016
- Spent nearly two decades producing unbuilt, visionary work before her first realized building (Vitra Fire Station, 1993)
- Developed "parametric design" with Patrik Schumacher — algorithms generate complex curved forms
- Key buildings: MAXXI Rome (2009), Heydar Aliyev Center Baku (2012), London Aquatics Centre (2012)
- First woman and first Arab to win the Pritzker Prize (2004) and RIBA Royal Gold Medal (2016)
- Influenced by Russian Suprematism, particularly Kazimir Malevich
- Controversial for spectacle-over-function critique and Qatar labour conditions
